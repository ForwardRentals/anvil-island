Quick Take

Anvil Island is the dark, anvil-shaped peak you stare at from the Sea to Sky Highway and the BC Ferries deck — rising straight out of Átl’ḵa7tsem (Howe Sound) with Leading Peak at its summit. There’s no ferry, no road, and no casual trailhead. Getting onto it, and up it, is a genuine small expedition, and that is the whole appeal.

Leading Peak is a steep, exposed scramble that tops out around 700 metres — effectively sea-level to summit in one go. From the top the view down the Sound is one of the best in the region: islands, ferries, the Tantalus behind, the Sky Pilot group across the water. It is a serious objective for experienced, self-sufficient parties, not a stroller hike.

The catch you need to know up front: much of Anvil Island is private land. Access is by boat, the land ownership is complicated, and the route is not a maintained public trail. This page is an honest orientation — not a green light. Do your homework on access before you plan a trip.

Getting There

You have to cross the Sound.

Your own boat

The traditional way: a private boat, kayak crossing for the experienced, or a friend with a runabout. Howe Sound water is cold and the wind comes up — treat the crossing as seriously as the climb.

No boat? We can help line up a charter across the Sound and a pickup for the way back. Tell us your date and group size and we’ll sort the logistics.

Honest Note

Read this before you plan a trip.

This is the most committing entry in the guide, and the one with the biggest asterisk. Much of Anvil Island is privately owned, the route up Leading Peak is not a maintained public trail, and the crossing is open, cold water that demands real boating judgment. None of that is a formality — confirm access and conditions before you go, and turn the day around if either isn’t right.

There is no cell certainty, no quick rescue, and no margin for a casual approach. Go with experience, leave a detailed plan with someone onshore, and respect that you are a guest — on private land and in Sḵwx̱wú7mesh (Squamish) Nation waters. Leave it exactly as you found it.

Questions & Answers

The questions people actually ask about Anvil Island.

01How do you get to Anvil Island?+

By boat only — there’s no ferry and no road. People reach it by private boat, by experienced sea-kayak crossing, or by charter. Howe Sound is cold and the wind builds through the day, so the crossing deserves as much planning as the climb. If you don’t have a boat, we can help arrange a charter crossing — or licensed skippers can rent a boat in Squamish and make the run themselves.

If landing isn’t the point, a guided boat tour of Howe Sound cruises past the island’s cliffs from Squamish.

02Can the public hike Leading Peak?+

This is the important one: much of Anvil Island is private land, and the route up Leading Peak is not a maintained public trail. Access is complicated and can change. Treat this page as an orientation, not permission — confirm current access before you plan a trip, and don’t assume.

03How hard is the climb?+

Hard. It’s roughly 700 metres of gain from sea level to the summit, steep throughout, with real exposure near the top — a scramble, not a walk. It suits fit, experienced parties who are comfortable with steep, unmaintained terrain and route-finding.

04How long does it take?+

Plan a full, committing day once you factor in the boat crossing both ways, the steep climb and descent, and a margin for weather and wind. This is a trip to start early and not rush, with turnaround times agreed in advance.

05Is it worth it?+

For the right party, the summit view over Átl’ḵa7tsem (Howe Sound) is among the best anywhere in the region — islands, ferries, the Tantalus and Sky Pilot ringing the water. But “worth it” assumes you’ve sorted access, the crossing, and the conditions. The view is the reward for doing the homework.

06When is the best time to go?+

Summer — roughly June through September, with August the most settled for both the climb and the Sound crossing. Watch the marine forecast as closely as the mountain one, and be willing to stand down if the water’s up.
